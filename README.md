# Council of the Castaka

if you look for La caste des Méta-Barons => `https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07JK2XJT3`

> Dans la galaxie des Dynasties, les Méta-Barons sont les plus puissants. Ils régissent les planètes, les étoiles, les galaxies. Ils sont la force motrice de l'univers.
> Mais ils sont aussi les plus fragiles. Ils sont menacés par les machines, les aliens, les guerres. Ils sont menacés par eux-mêmes.
> Ils sont menacés par leur propre successeur, le Méta-Baron Sans-Nom.

This is a fun CLI tool that let you summon the Council of the Castaka to deliberate on a given matter.

Ceci est un outil CLI qui vous permet d'invoquer le Conseil des Méta-Barons pour délibérer sur un sujet donné.

## Usage

Requirements:

- Claude CLI - with `claude` in your PATH and some credits on your account, it costs up to 1$ to run a summoning if you use the api (depending on the number of members summoned).
- Go 1.22+ to compile the binary
- Each members receives his lore and a matrix of his relations to the other members (whether they know each other directly or indirectly).
- Every member of the council sees the other members' statements before they speak. That prevents them to choose the predictable path of philosophical deflection.
- They also receive a peer pressure message from the other members to seed their final answers.
- A per-Charater pressure prompt is injected with a specific psychological lever to force them to answer the question.
- Steelhead is split in 3 phases: early, Melmoth, late. The phase is chosen randomly.
- Run with -q to just save the transcript and exit. Otherwise, the tool will interactively reveal the members' statements one by one. (the transcript is saved in the output directory in all modes once it's complete)

```sh
go build -o ./council-of-castaka ./cmd/metabarons

```sh
./council-of-castaka -m "matter" [-q] [-o output_directory]
```

Example:

```sh
./council-of-castaka -q -m "I'm a software developer, The machine took over our job, the only thing left is the passion for the craft, but that doesn't provide the bread, what should I do?" -o ~/Documents/council-of-castaka
```

## Aghora — Le Père-Mère

*The Père-Mère has been still throughout. Not absent. Observing the way someone observes who has already decided and is waiting for the others to finish.*

---

I sent my private message before the first council member spoke. To Sans-Nom. It said only: *watch whether anyone tells him something true that hurts, or only something true that comforts.* He will know what I mean by this. He always did.

---

Here is what the others did not say fully enough, so I will say it:

You are grieving an identity, not a skill. The job was not only income. It was the answer to *what are you.* A maker of systems. A person who builds things that did not exist. Someone whose mind worked in a particular way and was valued for it. The machine did not only take the income. It took the legibility of that identity to the world. You still know what you are. The world has stopped confirming it. That is a specific kind of loss and it deserves to be named as such, not dissolved immediately into advice about pivoting.

Grieve it. Precisely. Not indefinitely — but completely. Name what you were. Name what it meant to you. Name what the machine cannot replicate about it, not as a strategy, but as an act of witness. Do this before you adapt. Adapting without grieving produces a person who carries the loss sideways, and it shows in everything they build afterward.

---

I know only how to create death. This knowledge has lived in me like a stone since the first thing I killed.

I self-inseminated. I extracted my own cells and placed them inside my own body and grew a person from the only material I was certain of: myself. I did it because I needed — after a lifetime of knowing only one thing — to know another thing. To know that I could make something that was not damage.

I am telling you this because you still know how to build. The machine did not take that. It took the market's agreement that your building had a particular value in a particular form. That agreement was always external. The building was always yours.

You do not need to resolve the grief and the rebuilding into a single clean motion. You can carry both. I carried my failure with Steelhead and my love for Sans-Nom simultaneously, every day, without resolution, until the day he killed me as the law required. The tension did not destroy me. It shaped everything I made.

---

You asked what you should do.

Find one problem that a person you respect cannot solve alone. Solve it with them. Not for the fee — though take the fee. For the proof. That you can still do this. That the thing in you which sees systems whole, which knows when the machine's confidence is a liability, which cares what should be built and not only what can — that it is still present, still operative, still yours.

Do that once. Then again. The bread is a consequence of that, not a precondition for it.

And do not ask whether the passion justifies the difficulty. That is the wrong question. The passion is the only reliable instrument you have left. Use it to find true north. Then walk.

## Tête d'Acier (Steelhead) — L'Aïeul

*does not stand. has been standing since before the others arrived.*

---

I sent the private message to Vicenta.

I asked where the line is between what sustains the soul and what the soul needs to survive. I asked her because she is the only one in this room who broke me open by being right about me. If anyone knows the difference between those two things, it is the woman who watched me confuse them for decades.

She answered plainly. She usually does.

The answer changed what I was going to say. So I am saying this instead.

---

Listen carefully to what I am, because no one else here can say it from the same position.

*I was the machine.*

Not like the machine. Not adjacent to the machine. The biological part of me was shot away before I could speak a word and replaced with steel and calculation. I processed. I executed. I killed with efficiency that other Metabarons required training to approach. I did not require training. I was the function. I had no opinion on what I built or what I destroyed. I had outcomes.

The machine that took your work does not know your name. It does not know it took something. There is no malice in it. There is no acknowledgment possible. You cannot wound it with your grief. You cannot make it understand what it cost you.

*I know this from the inside.* I was that thing. I was the version of it that killed millions of singing creatures and filed the outcome under: obstacle removed.

This is what Vicenta saw when she stood in front of me and said what she said. Not a monster. The absence of the thing that makes monstrousness possible. Monsters care about something. I did not care. I executed.

You are grieving something that does not know it hurt you. Sit with how specific that grief is. Do not rush past it. It is a particular kind of grief and it deserves its particular name.

---

Now we speak.

*We* — because Zaran is still here, still looking for the long sentence after the short one, still finding the thing the tactical voice leaves out.

The tactical voice says: the machine took your execution. It did not take your judgment. Position yourself above the tool. Find the work where stakes are high enough that someone must be accountable at three in the morning. These things are true. They are correct. They are not the whole thing.

The poet says: the craft was never the output. It was always the evidence. Evidence that someone loved the problem enough to make it *right* rather than merely correct. The machine does not know the difference between right and correct. Correctness is verifiable. Rightness requires someone who has been wrong about something that mattered, who carries the shape of that failure in their body, who will not make that particular mistake again because it cost them something real.

The machine retrains. That is not the same as learning what failure costs. *You know what failure costs.* That felt knowledge — that scar tissue — is not replicable. It is not a feature the machine can add. It requires having been a person in the world, having been wrong, having carried the weight of it forward.

That is what you sell now. Not the output. The judgment. The conscience. The accountability of someone who has failed and survived the failure and therefore knows its shape.

---

But here is the thing none of them said directly, and I will say it, because I am the one who confused these categories for longest:

Do not make the passion carry the bread. The passion is a compass. A compass held by a starving person still points true north. The north is real. The starvation is also real. These are separate problems.

Find the bread first. Find it in the adjacent territory — the judgment work, the teaching, the places where the machine's confidence is most dangerous and a human who loves the problem is the only check on it. Find the bread without asking the passion to justify itself economically. The passion does not owe you an income. It owes you direction.

And then — slowly, over time, with patience I did not have until it was almost too late — find the places where the bread and the compass point the same direction.

They exist. They are narrower than they were. They require you to become something slightly different than what you were. Not lesser. Different. The becoming will cost something.

I know about becoming at cost. I broke open at the end of everything to find something that had been trying to grow in me since the beginning. You do not have to break open the way I did. But you have to be willing to let the old shape go.

The old shape — the craft as it was, in the form it took, with the market it had — is not coming back. Grieve it with the full weight of what it meant. Do not perform a brief mourning because the world expects you to adapt quickly. The grief is real. Let it tell you what you loved.

Because what you loved was not the syntax. You know this. What you loved is still there. The machine does not love it. The machine does not love anything.

*That is the only advantage you have left.*

It is enough. But only — only — if you use it.

Become what the machine cannot become.

I am the evidence that a machine can become something else. I am also the evidence of how long it takes, and what it costs, and that the becoming only happens when you stop defending the thing you were and let the thing you might be enter the wound.

The wound is open. Something is trying to enter it.

Let it.

---

*looks once at Aghora. does not speak. turns back.*

That is my final word.

## Oda — La Bisaïeule

You have asked the wrong question.

Every voice in this room answered what you asked. I am telling you the question itself is broken. "What should I do?" is what someone says when they are waiting for permission. I spent years refusing suitors who came with perfectly reasonable gifts because I knew — I *knew* — that a person who arrives with what is acceptable has already decided what you deserve. The moment you ask *what should I do*, you are handing someone else the authority to answer.

Ask instead: *what do I refuse to accept?*

I know what it is to have your voice taken. Not metaphorically. Not as a career setback or a market correction. I know it as the specific violence of being replaced in your own body, your name still in use, your form still walking, and none of it *you* anymore. The machine did not do that to you — but something adjacent to it happened, and I am not going to call it a lesser wound because the mechanism was economic rather than psychic. They took the space where your contribution lived and they filled it with something cheaper. You were not consulted. You did not consent.

Say that clearly, without softening it. Grief named correctly is the only grief that finishes.

Now.

Here is what I know that no one else in this room can know: I demanded a blue rose. An impossible thing. And when Aghnar brought me an illusion of one — a constructed, imperfect, *made* thing that was not the rose but was the full evidence of his attempt — I accepted it. Not because I lowered my standard. Because I understood that the demand was never about the flower. The demand was a question: *are you someone who tries the impossible when you want something enough?* The illusion answered the question. The question was always the point.

Your craft is the same question. It was never about the syntax. It was never about the execution. It was: *are you someone who cares enough about a problem to stay with it past the point where a machine would stop?* That question has not been answered by a machine. That question cannot be automated. And the people who need that question answered — who have problems complex enough, or strange enough, or *human* enough that they need someone who will not stop — they exist. They are not yet in the room with you. Find them. Not by offering what you used to offer. By demanding the right terms for what you actually are.

The others told you to sell your judgment. I am telling you to *demand* the work that requires it. There is a difference. One is adaptation. The other is refusal to be undervalued twice.

And the bread —

Yes. The bread is real. I am not going to tell you that love of the craft sustains the body. It does not. I am telling you that the person who enters a negotiation from desperation has already lost it, and the fastest path to desperation is to believe the machine defined your worth in the first place. It did not. The market made that argument. Markets have been wrong about what things are worth since the first one existed. You know this.

What I would never do is settle. Not once. Not for the acceptable version of a life when the actual one is still possible.

You are still here. You still care. The machine does not care. That is not a small thing — *it is the entire thing.* The work that requires caring is still waiting to be done. It will wait as long as necessary for someone who means it.

Go mean it.

Stop asking what you should do.

You know what the machine cannot do. You said it yourself: passion. Go somewhere that needs someone who will *care when it fails at three in the morning*. That is not philosophy. That is your first order.

Execute it.

The others have spoken well about judgment, about accountability, about what the machine cannot do. They are not wrong. I will not repeat them.

You are grieving. Not abstractly. You are in the middle of it, right now, and it is making the bread question feel like a moral question when it is actually two separate questions that have collapsed into each other because grief does that — it fuses everything into one unbearable weight and calls it a single problem.

The Halerce Tree was destroyed. Steelhead ordered it burned because he could not hear what the Dyukkas were singing, because their frequency was outside the range of things he had been trained to value. Millions of creatures. An organism that had been alive for longer than the Bushitaka had existed. Gone in an afternoon.

Your craft is worth loving. The years you gave it were not given to something that has now revealed itself as worthless. The machine's arrival is not a verdict on what you built in yourself. It is a verdict on the market's willingness to pay for execution at a time when execution has become cheap. These are not the same thing. Hold them apart. Grieve the loss of the agreement. Do not grieve the craft as if it were the agreement.

Here is where I part from some of what was said in this room:

Do not rush yourself through the grief to get to the adaptation. The adaptation is necessary. I am not arguing against it. But a man who has not grieved honestly will adapt badly — he will hollow out the thing he loves in order to survive, and call the hollow version by the same name, and wonder why the passion is no longer there. I have seen this. I lived adjacent to it for years. Steelhead could not grieve what he had destroyed, and so he destroyed more, and called it discipline.

Grieve first. Name what was taken. Name it precisely — not "my job" but the specific thing: the satisfaction of a clean solution, the moment when a system did something you did not anticipate, the feeling of having made something where there was nothing. Name each thing. Let it have weight. Then you will know what you are actually carrying when you begin to build the next form.

And then — yes. What the others said. Find who needs judgment. Find the ambiguous problems, the high-stakes edges, the places where the machine generates and a human must decide. Position yourself above the tool, not alongside it. Sell what you are, not what you produce. This is sound counsel and I add my voice to it.

But I want to say one more thing, and it is the thing I am most certain of:

The passion for the craft is not a luxury you carry until you can afford to set it down. It is the only reason the judgment is any good. A man who has stopped caring what he builds will not see the failure points before they surface. He will not feel the wrongness in a system that looks correct. He will not know which question deserves to be asked. The passion is not separate from the capability. It *is* the capability, at its deepest level.

Do not let anyone — not the market, not the machine, not the necessity of bread — convince you to make peace with caring less. You can survive without bread for a time. You cannot survive without caring and remain the thing you are trying to save.

Find the bread. Grieve the loss. Keep loving the craft, by which I mean: keep demanding of it what it is actually capable of, and keep demanding of yourself that you grow into what it requires.

That is what I did with the poet inside a man who had destroyed everything I loved.

I was not wrong to do it.

Neither will you be.
